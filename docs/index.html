How a few thousand voters chose Britain's next Prime Minister

By Lucia Mackenzie

Andy Burnham is on the cusp of becoming British Prime Minister with the backing of a tiny fraction of the electorate.

Affectionately known as King of the North, Burnham is a Labour party politician and has been Mayor of Greater Manchester for almost a decade.

While his popularity soared, current Prime Minister Keir Starmer faced fierce pressure to resign to make way for a new leader.

One Labour politician even stepped down from his job as a Member of Parliament to trigger a by-election.

The plan: have Burnham take over his seat and launch a formal leadership challenge.

Here is what happened next.

On June 18 2026, the people of Makerfield, a small area in North West England went to the polls.

Almost 25,000 of them voted Andy Burnham into Parliament, clearing the way for him to challenge Starmer as Prime Minister.

Throughout this story, every square represents 5,000 people.

Burnham won almost 55% of the votes cast, a decisive victory which ultimately prompted Starmer to resign a few days later.

While he won the vote with a comfortable majority, less than 60% of registered voters went to the polls.

That means Burnham technically has the backing of about a third of Makerfield's electorate.

The Makerfield by-election win gives Burnham his personal mandate to represent the constituency in Parliament.

Burnham has already ruled out calling an imminent general election to win a mandate to run the country at large.

Instead, he is relying on the Labour Party's general election victory in 2024 to bolster his democratic credentials.

In 2024, the Labour party won the support of 9.7 million voters across the U.K.

Burnham has promised to follow the mandate presented to voters during that election to justify becoming leader without the entire country's backing.

As Prime Minister, Burnham will wield significant power.

Labour won over a third of the vote in 2024, and with Britains first-past-the-post-electoral system that translated to a large majority of seats in Parliament.

Around 28.8 million valid votes were cast in the general election, meaning less than 60% of people registered to vote participated in the election.

That leaves Burnham likely to become Prime Minister based on the votes of less than 1 percent of the electorate.



This is not the first time a Prime Minister has taken power from an unpopular predecessor between general elections: Britain has seen six Prime Ministers and three general elections in the last decade.

Most recently, Rishi Sunak, the Conservative party leader and Prime Minister before the 2024 general election ran unopposed to take over after Liz Truss' rapid resignation.

Andy Burnham is now cruising towards a similar situation.

If no other Labour politician gets the support of enough lawmakers to be put on a leadership contest ballot by July 15, he will be appointed Labour Party leader, and, with the King's blessing, Prime Minister a matter of days later.
